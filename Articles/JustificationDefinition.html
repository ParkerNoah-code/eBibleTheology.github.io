Justification

Old Testament Background

Hebrew Terms

The primary Hebrew root that conveys the idea of justification is צָדַק (tsadaq), which means "to be righteous" or "to declare righteous." Several related words expand upon this concept:

  • צֶדֶק (tsedeq) – Righteousness or justice, often used to describe God's just nature.
  • צְדָקָה (tsedaqah) – Righteousness or justification, often associated with divine justice and human moral conduct.
  • מִשְׁפָּט (mishpat) – Judgment or justice, frequently appearing in legal and covenantal contexts.

These terms are used in judicial settings, where a person can be either justified (declared righteous) or condemned (declared guilty).

Legal and Judicial Contexts

One of the most significant aspects of justification in the Old Testament is its connection to legal proceedings. This forensic aspect is evident in passages that discuss courtroom settings, where a judge pronounces a verdict:

Deuteronomy 25:1 – "If there is a dispute between men and they come into court, and the judges decide between them, acquitting (justifying) the innocent and condemning the guilty."

This passage clearly illustrates that justification is a judicial declaration rather than an inherent moral state. A judge declares someone righteous not because of their personal virtue but because the legal system recognizes their innocence or absolves them of guilt.

Proverbs 17:15 – "He who justifies the wicked and he who condemns the righteous are both alike an abomination to the LORD."

Here, justification is depicted in contrast with condemnation. Justification is proper when it aligns with truth and justice; however, when the wicked are justified (i.e., declared righteous without cause), it is considered detestable to God.

Isaiah 5:23 – "Who acquit (justify) the guilty for a bribe, and deprive the innocent of his right!"

This passage emphasizes the perversion of justice when justification is granted improperly. These passages reinforce the legal framework of justification, demonstrating that it is a status declared by an authority, rather than an internal transformation.

Justification and Covenant

While the legal aspect of justification is crucial, it is also deeply connected to God’s covenant with Israel. The Old Testament consistently portrays God as the just and righteous King who maintains a covenantal relationship with His people.

Genesis 15:6 – "And he believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness."

This verse is foundational to the concept of justification by faith. Abraham was not justified by his works but by his faith in God's promises. Paul later uses this verse in Romans 4:1-5 to argue that justification is by faith apart from works.

Isaiah 53:11 – "By his knowledge shall the righteous one, my servant, make many to be accounted righteous, and he shall bear their iniquities."

This messianic prophecy foreshadows how justification will ultimately come through the atoning work of the Suffering Servant (Jesus Christ). The idea of substitutionary atonement is present here: the righteous one bears the sins of others so that they may be justified.

Psalm 143:2 – "Enter not into judgment with your servant, for no one living is righteous before you."

This verse acknowledges that no one can be justified before God based on their own righteousness. Justification must come through divine grace rather than human effort.

Thus, justification in the Old Testament is tied to God’s covenant faithfulness. Israel was expected to walk in obedience, but their justification ultimately depended on God's mercy and His provision of righteousness.

Justification and Atonement

The sacrificial system in the Old Testament also plays a critical role in understanding justification. Since humanity is inherently sinful (Psalm 51:5, Ecclesiastes 7:20), God provided a means by which sins could be atoned for, allowing justification to take place.

Leviticus 17:11 – "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls."

The sacrificial system demonstrates that justification requires atonement. Blood sacrifices served as a means of covering sin and enabling righteousness before God.

Exodus 34:6-7 – "The LORD, the LORD, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness, keeping steadfast love for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, but who will by no means clear the guilty."

God is just and cannot ignore sin. Justification must be based on both God’s mercy and His justice.

The sacrificial system, though temporary, pointed forward to a greater atonement, which would provide final, salvific justification for the life to come (Hebrews 10:1-4). This is ultimately fulfilled in Christ’s atoning death.

The Prophetic Hope

The Old Testament prophets frequently anticipated a time when this true justification would be fully realized:

Jeremiah 23:6 – "In his days Judah will be saved, and Israel will dwell securely. And this is the name by which he will be called: ‘The LORD is our righteousness.’"

The future Messiah will bring righteousness and justification.

Habakkuk 2:4 – "The righteous shall live by his faith."

This verse, later quoted by Paul in Romans 1:17 and Galatians 3:11, emphasizes that righteousness (justification) leading to life is attained through faith.

Conclusion

The Old Testament presents justification as a legal declaration of righteousness, closely associated with God’s justice and covenantal faithfulness. It is rooted in forensic language, sacrificial atonement, and the necessity of faith. While the Law and sacrifices provided a temporary, earthly means of justification, they pointed toward a greater fulfillment in the coming Messiah, Jesus Christ. The foundation laid in the Old Testament is fully realized in the New Testament doctrine of justification by faith, demonstrating the continuity of God's redemptive plan throughout history.

New Testament Background

Greek Terms

The New Testament employs several Greek words that convey the concept of justification. These terms are largely forensic, meaning they are related to legal or judicial declarations:

  • Δικαιόω (dikaioō) – "To justify" or "to declare righteous"; used in a legal sense rather than an intrinsic moral transformation.
  • Δικαιοσύνη (dikaiosynē) – "Righteousness" or "justice"; often refers to the righteousness of God and the righteousness credited to believers.
  • Δίκαιος (dikaios) – "Righteous" or "just"; used to describe both God's character and the status of those who are justified.
  • Δικαίωσις (dikaiōsis) – "Justification"; a legal acquittal or declaration of righteousness.

The forensic nature of these terms aligns with the Old Testament concept of justification, confirming that justification is a judicial act rather than a process of moral improvement.

The Gospels

One of the clearest examples illustrating Jesus' view on justification is found in the Parable of the Pharisee and the Tax Collector in Luke 18:9-14. In this parable, Jesus intentionally contrasts two starkly different approaches to righteousness and standing before God.

The Pharisee, representative of religious pride and legalistic righteousness, confidently boasts of his moral accomplishments, meticulously highlighting his fasting and tithing, believing that these external actions earn him divine favor.

Conversely, the tax collector, considered by contemporary Jewish society to be among the worst sinners due to his association with Roman oppression and corruption, stands at a distance, unable to lift his eyes toward heaven, deeply aware of his sinful state. In humility and faith, he simply prays, "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" (Luke 18:13).

Jesus concludes the parable saying, "I tell you, this man [the tax collector] went down to his house justified rather than the other" (Luke 18:14). This parable strongly aligns with Paul's doctrine of justification by faith apart from works of the Law (Romans 3:28). Jesus reveals that righteousness before God is extended to those who trust wholly in God's mercy rather than their own merit.

Justification in James

While Paul teaches that justification is by faith alone, James 2:24 says, "You see that a person is justified by works and not by faith alone." This is no contradiction:

  • Paul speaks of justification before God (a legal declaration).
  • James speaks of justification before men (evidence of true faith).

I have an article exegeting that passage in James in detail: Justification In James. Paul himself argues that same point using different language in numerous places and affirms that genuine faith produces works:

Galatians 5:6 – "For in Christ Jesus neither circumcision nor uncircumcision counts for anything, but only faith working through love."

Ephesians 2:10 – "For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works."

Thus, Paul, in full agreement with James, does not dismiss works; rather, he teaches that good works follow from a person with saving faith and demonstrate the veracity of their faith, but that good works are by no means the basis of justification. Justification is through faith alone.

Pauline Justification

Justification in Paul

The Apostle Paul develops the doctrine of justification most fully, especially in Romans, Galatians, and Philippians. His writings emphasize that justification is by faith apart from works of the Law, is based on Christ’s atoning work, and results in peace with God and freedom from condemnation.

Paul’s concept of justification is fundamentally legal and declarative, rather than intrinsic or transformative. This means that justification is not about making a person morally righteous but about the being declared righteous before God. The key Greek term used by Paul is δικαιόω (dikaioō), which means “to declare righteous” rather than “to make righteous.”

Forensic Nature

Paul’s use of δικαιόω (dikaioō) in these contexts confirms that justification is a legal pronouncement rather than an internal transformation (which is part of sanctification).

Romans 8:33-34 – "Who shall bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn?"

Here, justification is depicted as the opposite of condemnation in a courtroom setting. Just as a judge pronounces a defendant “not guilty,” God declares the believer righteous.

Romans 4:5 – "And to the one who does not work but believes in him who justifies the ungodly, his faith is counted as righteousness."

God is said to "justify the ungodly," reinforcing that justification is an act of divine grace, not human effort.

Galatians 2:16 – "Yet we know that a person is not justified by works of the law but through faith in Jesus Christ."

Justification is explicitly contrasted with works of the law, showing that righteousness is credited by faith, not earned.

Faith Alone: Romans 3:21-28

Romans 3:21 – "But now the righteousness of God has been manifested apart from the law."

Righteousness comes apart from the law, meaning it is not based on human merit.

Romans 3:22-23 – "For there is no distinction: for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."

Every person is in need of justification, as all are guilty before God.

Romans 3:24-25 – "And are justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus."

Justification is a free gift, based on Christ’s atoning work, not human performance.

Romans 3:28 – "For we hold that one is justified by faith apart from works of the law."

Justification is explicitly by faith, not by works.

Paul reinforces this point in Galatians 2:16, where he states three times that justification does not come by the law but by faith in Jesus Christ.

Abraham: Romans 4:1-5

Paul illustrates justification by faith using Abraham, quoting Genesis 15:6:

"Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness" (Romans 4:3).

Paul argues that Abraham was justified before circumcision, proving that righteousness comes by faith, not by adherence to the law. This means that faith has always been the means of justification, even in the Old Testament.

Atonement

Paul ties justification directly to the atoning work of Jesus Christ. Justification is not arbitrary but is based on Christ’s sacrificial death, which satisfies God's justice.

Romans 5:9 – "Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God."

Justification is possible only through Christ’s death, which serves as the basis for righteousness.

2 Corinthians 5:21 – "For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."

Christ bore our sin on the cross, so that by believing in Him we are pardoned from our sins and receive His righteousness instead.

Freedom from Condemnation

Paul frequently contrasts justification and condemnation, showing that justification leads to eternal life, while condemnation leads to judgment.

Romans 5:18 – "As one trespass led to condemnation for all men, so one act of righteousness leads to justification and life for all men."

Adam’s sin brought condemnation; Christ’s righteousness brings justification.

Romans 8:1 – "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."

Justification results in permanent freedom from condemnation. Justification is thus final and irreversible—those whom God has justified will not be condemned.

Eschatological Aspect

While Paul teaches that believers are already justified, he also suggests a future aspect of justification:

Romans 2:13 – "For it is not the hearers of the law who are righteous before God, but the doers of the law who will be justified."

This refers to the final vindication of the justified at the last judgment.

Galatians 5:5 – "For through the Spirit, by faith, we ourselves eagerly wait for the hope of righteousness."

There is an already-but-not-yet dimension to justification.

This shows that while justification is a present reality, it will be fully revealed on the Day of Judgment.

Doctrine of Justification

Definition

Justification is a forensic (legal) act of God in which He declares a person righteous based on the righteousness of Christ. It does not refer to an internal moral transformation (which is the role of sanctification) but to a change in legal status before God.

God declares sinners righteous by grace through faith in Christ alone. It is based on Christ’s atoning work, received by faith, and results in peace with God and eternal security. While good works follow as evidence of a transformed life, they are not the cause of justification. This doctrine underscores the grace of God, ensuring that salvation is a gift, not a reward.

Key aspects about what justification is and is not:

  • Declarative, not Transformative – Justification does not mean that a person is made righteous in behavior, but rather that God declares them righteous.
  • Imputed, not Earned – The righteousness given in justification is imputed (credited to the believer) rather than earned by human effort.
  • By Faith, Not Works – Justification is received through faith alone, apart from the works of the Law (Romans 3:28).
  • Based on Christ’s Work – Justification is possible because of Jesus’ substitutionary atonement and perfect righteousness (2 Corinthians 5:21).

Basis of Justification

Justification is not an arbitrary act of God but is based on the substitutionary atonement of Jesus Christ. Since God is perfectly just, He cannot declare sinners righteous without a sufficient basis. The atonement provides that basis.

Romans 5:9 – "Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God."

Christ’s blood (His death) is the means by which justification is secured.

2 Corinthians 5:21 – "For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."

This is the great exchange: Christ takes our sin, and we receive His righteousness.

Hebrews 9:22 – "Without the shedding of blood, there is no forgiveness of sins."

Justification is impossible without Christ’s sacrificial death.

Thus, justification is not based on human effort but on the finished work of Christ, whose righteousness is imputed to the believer.

Means of Justification

Paul repeatedly emphasizes that justification is received through faith alone, apart from works.

Romans 5:1 – "Therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ."

Ephesians 2:8-9 – "For by grace you have been saved through faith, and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."

Galatians 3:11 – "Now it is evident that no one is justified before God by the law, for 'The righteous shall live by faith.'"

Faith is not a work but the means by which justification is received. It is a trusting reliance on Christ rather than an effort to earn righteousness.

Justification and Works

While justification is by faith alone, true faith will result in a transformed life.

Ephesians 2:10 – "For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works."

Justification is not on the basis of works, but works are the evidence of true faith.

Thus, works do not contribute to justification, but they follow from it as proof of a transformed life.

Assurance and Permanence

Since justification is based on Christ’s work, it is secure and irreversible.

Romans 8:1 – "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."

Romans 8:33-34 – "Who shall bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn?"

John 10:28-29 – Jesus promises that no one can snatch believers from His hand.

Justification is once for all time and permanent, as it is based on Christ’s righteousness, not human effort.
