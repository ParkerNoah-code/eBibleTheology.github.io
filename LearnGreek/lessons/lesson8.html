Lesson 8: Prepositions

Greek Prepositions

  • ἐν – in
  • ἐκ (ἐξ) – out of, from
    ἐκ before consonant, ἐξ before vowel
  • διά + genitive – through
    δι’ before vowel
  • ὑπό – under
    ὑπ’ before consonant, ὑφ’ before vowel + rough breathing
  • σύν – with
  • παρά – from, beside
    παρ’ before vowel
  • μετά – with, after
    μετ’ before consonant, μεθ’ before vowel + rough breathing
  • ἀπό – from
    ἀπ’ before consonant, ἀφ’ before vowel + rough breathing
  • εἰς – into
  • ἐπί – upon, on
    ἐπ’ before consonant, ἐφ’ before vowel + rough breathing
  • κατά – according to
    κατ’ before consonant, καθ’ before vowel + rough breathing
  • περί – no elision
  • πρό – before (no elision)

Form Rules

  • Prepositions ending in a vowel drop it before a word starting with a vowel (elision).
  • If the preposition ends in π or τ and the next word’s initial vowel has a rough breathing mark, the consonant changes to φ or θ.
    Examples:
    ἀφ’ ἡμερῶν – from the days
    μεθ’ ἡμέρας – after days

Usage Principles

  • The object of a preposition is never in the nominative case.
  • Do not translate the genitive with "of," nor the dative with "to" or "for."
  • Adverbial prepositional phrases modify verbs (answer: where, when, how, why).
  • Adjectival prepositional phrases modify nouns (answer: which, what kind).
  • Prepositions may be repeated for stylistic emphasis (e.g., πῦρ ἐκ πορεύεται ἐκ τοῦ στόματος αὐτῶν – fire came out of their mouth).
  • Nouns/adjectives following prepositions are often definite, even without an article (e.g., ἀπὸ κυρίου – from the Lord).
  • New Testament Greek shows increased preposition usage for clarity (e.g., ἐν τῇ τρίτῃ ἡμέρᾳ – on the third day).
